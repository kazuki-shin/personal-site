From Simulation to World Models

VLAs generalize through semantics. World models generalize through dynamics. The best labs are converging on using both — and NVIDIA already shipped the proof.

The question isn't whether world models and VLAs are in tension. They're not. The real question is: where does generalization come from?

VLAs bet on semantics. World models bet on dynamics. The best labs are converging on using both — world models generate the training data that VLAs learn from. You get language grounding from the backbone and causal reasoning from the dynamics model. You stop needing proportionally more real-world demonstrations to scale.

Two bets on generalization

VLAs — Pi0, OpenVLA — are fine-tuned vision-language models that output actions. Internet-scale pretraining bakes in world knowledge; robot control adapts it. Generalization comes from the backbone. The weakness: brittle on novel physics, hungry for real-world demonstration data.

World models predict how state changes in response to action — a compressed internal model of cause and effect. Dreamer does this in latent space: plan in imagination, execute in reality. Sample-efficient. Capable of counterfactual reasoning. The weakness: hard to scale, and the model can hallucinate dynamics.

The scaling question

Transformers scaled because the objective matched the data: next-token prediction on internet text is infinitely scalable, self-supervised, universal. The task and the corpus were made for each other.

World models don't have that pairing yet. They need interaction data — state, action, next state, outcome — which is scarce, domain-specific, expensive. There is no internet of physical dynamics.

Defining a universal task function as simple and elegant as next-token prediction in LLMs has long been a central goal of world model research.

Fei-Fei Li, building World Labs, is betting that video at internet scale is the unlock. The architectures are nascent. The scaling laws aren't established. But the direction is clear.

The transition I watched at NVIDIA

NVIDIA's synthetic data pipeline for AV and robotics used to run through Omniverse — physics-based simulation, game engine style. Hand-authored 3D scenes, specified materials, rendered training data. It worked. It was also fundamentally hand-crafted: labor-intensive, limited in diversity, slow to scale.

Cosmos replaced that entire pipeline. Trained on 20 million hours of real-world video, it generates physics-aware synthetic data from text, images, or structured inputs. The Cosmos paper puts it cleanly: physical AI needs a digital twin of itself (the policy model) and a digital twin of the world (the world model). Omniverse simulated the world. Cosmos models it.

Compose a 3D scene in Omniverse as ground truth. Condition Cosmos to generate photorealistic variants — weather, lighting, edge cases — at scale. Days become hours. Figure AI, Agility Robotics, 1X, and Skild AI are already using it.

The shift: you no longer author world states. The model generates them.

This is what Fei-Fei means by closing the sim-to-real gap. It's not a research direction. It shipped.

The same insight, applied to patients

LeCun and Alex LeBrun (Nabla co-founder) launched AMI Labs with the same framing applied to healthcare: LLMs predict words, world models predict patients. A physician mentally simulates treatment paths before acting — fluids versus vasopressors, admit versus discharge. That internal model is what AI needs to replicate.

At Kaigo, we sit on longitudinal patient event streams: clinical events, interventions, outcomes. Already causally structured. Already sequential. Already labeled. A world model over patient trajectories would let you plan care coordination sequences — not prompt an LLM for the next step, but simulate the next thirty days.

The data scarcity problem that plagues general robotics is tractable in a bounded clinical domain. You don't need a general world model. You need one that predicts readmissions and care gap closure for a specific ACO population. That's a data flywheel moat, not a research problem. Probably why LeCun chose healthcare as a proving ground.

The underlying pattern

Robots manipulating objects. Care coordinators deciding who to call. The structure is identical: long-horizon sequential decisions under partial observability. LLMs handle this by generating plausible next steps. World models handle it by simulating consequences.

Simulating is harder. It's also the only way to get reliability. The question is which domains accumulate enough causally-structured data to make it work — and build around it before anyone else does.
