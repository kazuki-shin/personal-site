Why I'm Building in Healthcare

The healthcare system is fundamentally broken — not because people don't care, but because the infrastructure was never designed for the people it serves.

I didn't start my career in healthcare. I started behind a camera lens, shooting runway shows at New York Fashion Week, trying to capture something beautiful and fleeting. But somewhere along the way, I realized that the most important problems weren't on the runway — they were in the waiting rooms.

The problem is structural

Healthcare in the United States is a $4.3 trillion industry that still runs on fax machines. The average patient waits 26 days to see a specialist. Caregivers — the invisible backbone of our system — are burning out at unprecedented rates. And the technology that exists to help them is often more burden than benefit.

I founded Kaigo Health because I believe the next generation of healthcare technology shouldn't just digitize paperwork. It should fundamentally rethink how care is delivered, coordinated, and experienced.

Why now

Three forces are converging that make this the right moment to build: AI has reached a level of capability that can genuinely augment clinical decision-making. The regulatory environment is shifting toward interoperability and patient data access. And a new generation of healthcare workers is demanding better tools.

The best time to fix healthcare was twenty years ago. The second best time is now.

At Kaigo, we're starting with the people who need it most — the caregivers, the coordinators, the people who hold the system together with duct tape and compassion. We're building the infrastructure they deserve.

If you're an engineer, designer, or operator who wants to work on problems that actually matter — I'd love to hear from you.
