Why I'm Building in Healthcare

The healthcare system is fundamentally broken — not because people don't care, but because the infrastructure was never designed for the people it serves.

I didn't start my career in healthcare. I started behind a camera lens, shooting runway shows at New York Fashion Week, trying to capture something beautiful and fleeting. But somewhere along the way, I realized that the most important problems weren't on the runway — they were in the waiting rooms.

The problem is structural

Healthcare in the United States is a $4.3 trillion industry that still runs on fax machines. The average patient waits 26 days to see a specialist. Caregivers — the invisible backbone of our system — are burning out at unprecedented rates. And the technology that exists to help them is often more burden than benefit.

I founded Kaigo Health because I believe the next generation of healthcare technology shouldn't just digitize paperwork. It should fundamentally rethink how care is delivered, coordinated, and experienced.

Why now

Three forces are converging that make this the right moment to build: AI has reached a level of capability that can genuinely augment clinical decision-making. The regulatory environment is shifting toward interoperability and patient data access. And a new generation of healthcare workers is demanding better tools.

The best time to fix healthcare was twenty years ago. The second best time is now.

At Kaigo, we're starting with the people who need it most — the caregivers, the coordinators, the people who hold the system together with duct tape and compassion. We're building the infrastructure they deserve.

If you're an engineer, designer, or operator who wants to work on problems that actually matter — I'd love to hear from you.

Leverage and the Modern Founder

Naval Ravikant wrote that you should "give society what it wants but does not yet know how to get — at scale." Media is the newest form of leverage.

I recently sat next to a founder at a dinner who told me his first lawyer came from Stripe, his first designer was world-class, and he raised a $10M seed — all because people read his blog. No warm intros. No networking events. Just writing.

That conversation changed how I think about building a company.

The four forms of leverage

Naval Ravikant identifies four forms of leverage: labor (people working for you), capital (money working for you), code (software working for you), and media (content working for you). The first two require permission. The last two don't.

Code and media are the great equalizers. A first-time founder with a blog can reach more people than a partner at a top VC firm. A well-written essay about your vision can do more for recruiting than a hundred LinkedIn messages.

Writing as a founder's superpower

When you write publicly about what you're building and why, several things happen simultaneously. You attract people who share your vision. You repel people who don't — which is equally valuable. You clarify your own thinking. And you create a permanent, searchable artifact of your ideas that compounds over time.

Writing is not marketing. Writing is thinking, made visible.

The founders I admire most — the ones building truly enduring companies — almost all write. Not because they have time. Because they can't afford not to.

This is why I'm starting to write. Not to build an audience for its own sake, but because the act of articulating what Kaigo Health is building forces me to think more clearly about it. And if that thinking resonates with someone exceptional — an engineer, an investor, a future patient — then the writing has done its job.

January 2026

What Fashion Photography Taught Me About Product Design

Standing behind a lens at NYFW, I learned that the best designs share something with the best products — they make complexity feel effortless.

People are always surprised when I tell them I was a runway photographer. "How did you go from fashion to healthcare?" they ask. But the transition made more sense than you'd think.

The art of seeing

Photography is fundamentally about seeing — about training your eye to notice what others miss. The way light falls on a shoulder. The moment a model's expression shifts from composed to vulnerable. The one frame in a thousand that tells the whole story.

Building products requires the same kind of seeing. You have to observe how people actually use things, not how they say they do. You have to notice the moments of friction, confusion, and delight. You have to find the one insight in a thousand that unlocks the whole product.

Constraints breed creativity

At NYFW, you have perhaps three seconds to capture each look. The lighting is fixed. The angle is constrained by a hundred other photographers. You can't ask the model to stop. Within those brutal constraints, you have to find something extraordinary.

Startups operate under similar constraints — limited time, limited money, limited people. The best founders, like the best photographers, don't resent constraints. They use them as creative fuel.

Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away. — Antoine de Saint-Exupéry

At Kaigo, we design with this principle. Every screen, every interaction, every feature has to earn its place. If it doesn't make the caregiver's life meaningfully better, it doesn't ship.
